## Some Birding Starter Tips

There are lots of different kinds of birders.
If you've ever watched birds in your backyard or at the park, you are a birder in my book.
There are also some intense birders like those comically shown in the documentary Listers.
While I enjoy trying to find new species, I'm happy even when people just notice birds that excite them.
So even if you don't have a pair of binoculars, or can't distinguish [crows from ravens](/caw/), birds can still be fun to watch.

But if you would like some quick tips for how to get more into birding, I have a few recommendations.

### Download the Merlin App

Before cell phones, I would thumb through bird field guide books to learn different species and it was hard to tell which birds were common on my area.
Now I use the Merlin app as one of my primary birding tools.
It can listen to bird songs and tell you what birds are singing around you.
If you have a photo, you can upload it to the app and it will give you some possible identifications.
Even without a photo, you can answer a few questions to try to identify a bird and compare with reference photos and descriptions.
You can even explore a list of birds likely in your area based on your GPS location and the time of the year, helping narrow down which birds might be migrating through or who your local resident species are.
If I were to make one technical recommendation to start birding, it'd be to try out this app.

### Dig up a pair of binoculars

Optics can be one of the most expensive pieces of birding equipment, but even a cheap pair of binoculars can be helpful when you're trying to distinguish features of a bird up in a tree.
Any pair of binoculars can be helpful, so see if you can find that old pair hidden in your home.
If you do want to buy a pair, you can start with something under $100.
I'd recommend starting with something with 8x magnification as that helps with visibility without being too narrow a view that it becomes hard to track birds.
I recently bought a $200 pair of binoculars from Vortex that I have liked.
But you can also find more affordable or kid-friendly pairs from retailers like Bushnell.

### Learn some basic field marks

When you start noticing birds, it can sometimes get frustrating not being able to distinguish them all.
It happens to me all the time.
There will be an odd looking bird that could be something I've never seen before, but it's too hard to track to be sure.
Especially when getting started, find a bird who's less shy and see what you notice.
When you pay attention, there are lots of clues to who the bird is:

- What color are it's feathers?
- What patterns do you see on it's feathers?
- What color are it's bill and legs?
- What shape is it's body?
- Is it big or small?
- What shape is it's bill and how big is it compared to it's body?
- What is it doing?
- What sounds is it making?
- How does it fly?
- What habitat does it hanging out in?
- How is it interacting with other birds?

Each of these can solve the mystery of what bird you are looking at.
When you have a mental image of your bird, compare that with your field guide, like the Merlin app.
Do the photos match what you see?
Notice how different species differ between male and female or winter and summer plumage?
Practice these skills on something simple and work your way up to more difficult challenges.

### Identify something YOU find interesting

While the birding world is often dominated by lists of species, I think it's more important to notice the birds themselves.
Is there a particular bird you find strikingly beautiful?
Did a bird surprise you?
Did you see a cool behavior?
All of these things make a bird interesting, no matter how common.
By focusing on the aspects of birds you enjoy, you'll get more out of birding than by identifying species alone.
